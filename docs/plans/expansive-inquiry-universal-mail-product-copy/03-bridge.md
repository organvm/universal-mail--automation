## Five Bridges

- Airport security
  - Structural analogy: most bags should move quickly, but flagged items require special handling and a recorded chain of custody.
  - Product implication: position the product as "security screening for inbox cleanup," not "AI sorting."
  - Hybrid question: What would the landing page look like if every email had either a safe-to-move stamp or a hold-at-gate stamp?

- Emergency room triage
  - Structural analogy: the ER does not solve every problem first; it separates "must act now" from "can wait" without killing the patient.
  - Product implication: the Eisenhower tiers are less important as a named framework than as a visible promise: the urgent things stay visible.
  - Hybrid question: Can the demo show "what stays in front of you" before showing "what gets cleaned away"?

- Museum conservation
  - Structural analogy: restoration removes dirt without damaging the original artifact, and every intervention is documented.
  - Product implication: the receipt becomes conservation notes, not compliance jargon.
  - Hybrid question: What if the copy said "we clean around the important stuff" instead of "we automate triage"?

- Surgical checklist
  - Structural analogy: the checklist is boring, but it prevents catastrophic omission during routine action.
  - Product implication: the protected sender gate is a pre-flight checklist: court? bank? government? account? client? If yes, do not move.
  - Hybrid question: Could the product expose the safety checklist as the main demo instead of exposing the technical receipt first?

- Pawn shop intake ledger
  - Structural analogy: every item that enters or leaves is written down because value and dispute risk are built into the domain.
  - Product implication: the audit receipt is useful because email creates future disputes: "Did I receive it? Did I delete it? Where did it go?"
  - Hybrid question: What future argument does the product help the buyer win or avoid?

## Hybrid Questions

1. What are the protected categories that buyers recognize instantly, without learning the system?
2. What proof artifact would a nervous buyer actually understand at a glance?
3. Can the demo be organized around "safe to move / never move / needs review" instead of system internals?
4. What is the receipt's everyday name: cleanup receipt, proof slip, safety log, inbox receipt?
5. Which future regret does the product prevent?

## Most Productive Bridge

Museum conservation is the most productive bridge. It holds both halves: cleaning and preserving. It also explains why the proof layer matters without sounding like enterprise compliance theater. The product is not merely sorting email; it is cleaning a messy archive without damaging the records.
